This is not a cabin with a view. This is a home in the sky — suspended in a canopy of white oak and beech that has stood for over a century. You'll cross a thirty-foot rope bridge through the trees as a warm-up. Then the hundred-foot bridge appears — the one that makes your heart beat faster before the forest slows it back down. That one takes you to your front door. You'll fall asleep sixty feet in the air with nothing above you but stars and nothing below you but the creek rolling through the forest floor. And in the morning, you'll understand why no one who comes here leaves the same.

Built by Hand

A Family That Lives in the
Forest They Built

They carried every beam and board down four hundred feet of mountain by hand because they wouldn't let a machine touch the forest. Mick built the framing sixty feet in the air while Livy grew their first daughter. The arborists said the rope bridge was too long. They built it anyway. Two weeks. A hundred feet. Suspended over the valley.

They live here now — Mick, Livy, Aurora, and Tilly Mac. The girls think rope bridges are a normal way to get home. When you arrive, the people who designed and built your treehouse are the same people handing you a welcome basket.

That's Loxley. There's nowhere else like it because nobody else would build it this way.
