Our Story

He Didn't Build a Business.
He Built a Forest.

There's a man who lives on forty-two acres of hardwood cove forest in the North Georgia mountains. He's Canadian by birth, American by choice, and a builder by something deeper than either. He has a wife named Livy, two daughters who run through these woods like they own them (they do), and a collection of treehouses that hang fifty-five feet in the canopy of trees that were old when his grandparents were young.

His name is Mick. And he built all of it with his hands.

Not managed the build. Not oversaw the construction. Built it. He felled timber on this property. He milled it into beams. He engineered connections to living white oaks that flex and sway with the wind because a treehouse isn't a building — it's a conversation between wood and wood, between something built and something grown, between a man's vision and a forest's patience.

He hoisted those beams fifty-five feet into the air. He bolted them into trees that were saplings when soldiers marched through these mountains. He strung a hundred-foot rope bridge between two oaks and walked across it first to make sure it would hold. It held. It holds two hundred guests a year now, and the look on their faces when they cross it is the reason he built it.

Loxley Forest isn't a resort that was planned in a boardroom. There were no investors. No brand consultants. No focus groups. There was a man, a mountain, a growing family, and an unshakeable conviction that the world has enough hotels and not nearly enough places that make you feel something you can't find on a screen.

The name comes from the forest where Robin Hood made his stand — a place where ordinary people lived extraordinary lives among the trees. Mick liked that story. He liked the idea that a forest could be a home, a refuge, and an adventure all at once.

So he did.

When you stay at Loxley, the person who greets you is the person who built your room. The person who fires up the pizza oven is the person who laid the stone for the foundation. This isn't a brand story. It's just a story. And it's true.

Livy runs the things Mick doesn't notice — the details that turn a well-built structure into a place that feels like someone cares. The wildflowers on the table. The local honey in the TreeMail basket. The handwritten welcome note that says exactly the right thing.

Rory and Tilly Mac are growing up in a forest their father built. They think rope bridges are a normal way to get home. They think the sound of a creek at night is what silence sounds like. They think everyone's dad builds houses in the sky.

This is Loxley Forest. A family's home. A builder's masterwork. A place in the mountains that changes people in ways they don't fully understand until they're driving home and realize they left something behind — not their phone charger, but something heavier. Something they'd been carrying too long.

We're glad you found us. Come up. The forest is waiting.
